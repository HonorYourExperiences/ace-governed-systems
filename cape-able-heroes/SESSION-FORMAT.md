# Cape-Able Heroes WonderLab – Session Format (v1)

**Session Length:** 60–75 minutes  
**Group Size:** 6–20 children (optimal: 8–14)  
**Age Range:** 5–12 years (activities can be adjusted by theme and challenge difficulty)  
**Status:** v1 — field-tested once; use with theme-specific build packs for the next sessions

---

## Core Transformation

Every WonderLab session moves a child through one arc:

> **Hesitation → Participation**

The child does not need to feel ready. The child does not need to prove anything. The session is designed so that the first small step is always reachable — and completing it becomes evidence they carry with them.

---

## What the Child Leaves With

- A **Cape-Able Hero identity** (hero name + their declared strength)
- A **Hero Badge or Cape Element** (physical artifact — tangible proof)
- A **Hero Card** (personalized, names their challenge and what they did)
- A **Parent Reflection Card** (for the adult to witness and extend the moment at home)

---

## Session Flow

### Phase 1 — Arrival & World-Building (0–10 min)

**Purpose:** Create a distinct space. This is not a regular room. Something different is possible here.

**Facilitator actions:**
- Greet each child at the door by name (or introduce yourself and ask theirs)
- Use language that signals this is a different kind of experience:
  > *"This is the WonderLab. In here, we build Cape-Able Heroes. That means people who try things — even when it feels hard."*
- Set the one rule of the WonderLab:
  > *"The only rule here is: try. Perfection was never the point — it was never even required. That's all a Cape-Able Hero does."*
- Briefly introduce the theme (aviation, nature, building, etc.) with one image or prop to anchor the world

**Notes:**
- Do not praise children for being "smart" or "talented" on arrival — this sets conditional worth
- Acknowledge arrival: *"You showed up. That's the first step."*

---

### Phase 2 — Hero Identity Moment (10–25 min)

**Purpose:** Give each child an identity to step into before any challenge is attempted. Identity comes first.

**Facilitator actions:**
1. Explain what a Cape-Able Hero is — not a superhero, a real one:
   > *"A Cape-Able Hero is someone who feels scared or unsure, and tries anyway. They don't have to succeed the first time. They just have to begin."*

2. Give each child a **Hero Identity Card** (blank or partially pre-printed)
   - Ask them to write or say their **Hero Name** (can be their real name, a chosen name, or a title)
   - Ask them to choose or declare their **Cape-Able Strength** from a short list:
     - Bravery, Curiosity, Persistence, Kindness, Creativity, Focus, Problem-Solving
     - (Children can also name their own if they want)

3. Read each child's strength back to them clearly and sincerely:
   > *"[Name], your strength is [Strength]. We're going to use that today."*

4. Present the **Hero Badge or Cape Element** — not as a reward, but as a beginning:
   > *"You don't earn this at the end. You get it now — because you're already in the WonderLab, which means you already started."*

**Notes:**
- Let children who are hesitant watch briefly before deciding — do not force
- Have a co-facilitator or assistant move through the room and quietly support children who seem unsure
- Avoid language like "You're going to be amazing!" — prefer: *"Let's see what you can do."*

---

### Phase 3 — Challenge Activity (25–55 min)

**Purpose:** Give children a structured, small, achievable challenge tied to the theme and their hero identity. The challenge is designed so that *trying* is success.

**Themed Challenge Structure (applies to all themes):**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **The Mission** | A brief, story-framed challenge intro. 1–2 sentences. |
| **The Task** | One clear, hands-on activity. Completable in 15–25 minutes. |
| **The First Step** | The single smallest first move — always stated explicitly |
| **Support Available** | Tools, examples, or peer help — visible and reachable |
| **Completion Signal** | A clear moment when the child can recognize: "I did it" |

**Facilitation approach during the challenge:**
- Circulate and observe — use the Observation Tool
- When a child is stuck, ask: *"What's one small thing you could try next?"*
- When a child succeeds at any step, name it specifically:
  > *"You just figured that out on your own. That's what [their strength] looks like."*
- When a child says they can't: *"You haven't figured it out yet. What could you try?"*
- Do NOT take over. Do NOT solve it for them.

**For group challenges:** Assign small teams of 2–3. Each child still has an individual contribution.

**Themed Challenge Examples:**

- **Aviation:** Fold and test a paper airplane. Adjust it until it flies farther than your first attempt.
- **Nature:** Build a small habitat for an imaginary creature using materials provided. Explain one thing your creature needs to survive.
- **Engineering/Building:** Build a tower using limited materials that can hold a small weight for 5 seconds.
- **Storytelling:** Create a 3-panel story about a Cape-Able Hero who solves one problem using your declared strength.
- **Mapping:** Draw a map from "Where I Am Now" to "One Thing I Want to Try" — using your strength as the path.

---

### Phase 4 — Reflection & Recognition (55–70 min)

**Purpose:** Convert the experience into explicit evidence. The child leaves knowing what they did, not just that they had fun.

**Facilitator actions:**
1. Bring the group back together
2. Ask 2–3 children to share one thing: *"What was one moment when you kept going even though it was hard?"*
3. Complete the **Hero Card** with each child (or have them complete it themselves):
   - *"My challenge was..."*
   - *"I kept going when..."*
   - *"My Cape-Able strength helped me by..."*
4. Formal recognition moment — say each child's name and strength clearly:
   > *"[Name] is a Cape-Able Hero. Their strength is [Strength] and today they [one specific thing they did]."*
5. Present the completed **Hero Card** and remind them what it means:
   > *"This isn't a trophy for winning. It's evidence. You tried, and you have proof."*

**Notes:**
- Keep the recognition moment sincere and specific — not generic praise
- Allow quiet children to be recognized for observation, small steps, or support given to others

---

### Phase 5 — Close & Parent Handoff (70–75 min)

**Purpose:** Extend the experience beyond the room. Give parents the language to continue it.

**Facilitator actions:**
1. Close the session with one sentence to the children:
   > *"Every Cape-Able Hero started exactly where you started — not knowing if they could. Now you know you can try. That's the most important thing."*
2. Distribute **Parent Reflection Cards** (see Take-Home Kit)
3. If parents are present, brief them in 2 minutes:
   > *"Your child did something hard today. When you talk about it tonight, ask them: 'What was the hard part?' and 'What did you try?' That's more powerful than 'Did you have fun?'"*

---

## Facilitator Reminders

- **You are not rescuing anyone.** You are creating conditions for children to rescue themselves.
- **Name what you see, not what you feel.** "I see you kept trying" not "I'm so proud of you."
- **Silence is okay.** Children thinking is not a problem to solve.
- **A child who doesn't finish is not a failure.** Name what they did attempt.
- Use the Observation Tool throughout to capture real field evidence.

---

## Theme Modularity

Every theme plugs into the same structure. To add a new theme:

1. Write the **Mission Intro** (1–2 sentences, story-framed, ties to theme world)
2. Define the **Task** (one hands-on activity, completable in 15–25 min)
3. State the **First Step** explicitly
4. List **Materials Needed**
5. Define the **Completion Signal**

Store each theme as a separate section in the CHALLENGE-CARDS file or as a standalone theme card.
