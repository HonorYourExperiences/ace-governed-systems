## Session 2 Test Objective

Test whether explicit aviation roles, crew staffing, first-fold support, mechanic entry, and payload design increase participation and produce clearer evidence of capability.

Do not treat this pack as proven curriculum yet. Treat it as the next field-test configuration.

---

## Materials

- Blank paper and printed aircraft sheets
- Markers, crayons, pencils
- Role cards, one set per crew
- Crew staffing sheet, one per crew
- Payload material: small colored paper scraps, lightweight strips, tape, stickers
- Rubber mallet or other safe mechanic prop
- Flight test observation sheet
- Take-home airline kit sheet
- Optional target zone or runway marker

Safety note: mechanic props must be light, blunt, and used only on the table surface or paper aircraft, never on people or furniture.

---

## Role Cards

Print or write one card for each role. Children choose, draw, or are invited into a role before the build starts.

| Role | Cape-ability | What this role owns | Starter prompt |
|------|--------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Pilot | Focus | Flies the aircraft and reports what happened | "What did the aircraft do in the air?" |
| Air Traffic Controller | Awareness | Helps direct launch order and flight path | "Where should this aircraft fly safely?" |
| Aircraft Mechanic | Precision | Checks folds, secures creases, and repairs damage | "What needs to be tightened, fixed, or checked?" |
| Airport Safety Officer | Judgment | Keeps the crew careful and aware of space | "Is this launch safe and ready?" |
| Ground Support | Support | Helps teammates stay included and ready | "Who needs help getting back into the mission?" |
| Aerospace Engineer | Improvement | Chooses one design change after each flight | "What should we change before the next test?" |
| Airline Founder | Imagination | Names the airline, mission, and destination | "Where does this airline go, and why?" |
| Payload Designer | Mission | Chooses what the aircraft carries, releases, delivers, or represents | "What is your aircraft carrying?" |

Facilitator rule: if a child resists one role, offer another. The point is belonging somewhere in the system, not forcing a preferred role.

## First-Fold Script

Use this before any child is expected to fold independently.

1. Hold up the paper.
2. Say: "The first fold is where the aircraft begins. It does not have to be perfect. It has to begin."
3. Demonstrate one slow center fold.
4. Say: "Engineers practice precision. Nobody is born already precise."
5. Let children try once.
6. If a child hesitates, offer a role-based entry:
   - "Do you want to be the mechanic and secure the fold?"
   - "Do you want to be the test pilot and fly the first version?"
   - "Do you want to be the engineer who chooses the first change?"

Do not spend the room's energy forcing every child to fold the same way. Keep the mission moving.

---

## Mechanic Entry Protocol

Use this when a child wants to participate but is not ready to fold, design, or fly.

1. Facilitator or teammate makes the fold.
2. Place the folded edge flat on the table.
3. Hand the child the mechanic prop.
4. Say: "Mechanic, secure this fold."
5. Child taps along the crease.
6. Say: "This aircraft carries your work now."

Observation to capture: did the mechanic role move the child from watching to participating?

## Payload Station

Set aside a small station with lightweight materials.

Rules:

- Payload must be light enough to fly.
- Payload must stay inside or attached until launch.
- Payload can represent cargo, passengers, supplies, celebration, rescue, mail, or a made-up mission.
- If the aircraft flies differently, that is data.

Prompts:

- "What is your aircraft carrying?"
- "Who is it for?"
- "What should happen when it flies?"
- "Did the payload change the flight?"

Evidence target: capture at least three payload/mission examples before changing the station design.
